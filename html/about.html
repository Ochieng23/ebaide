About me

Hi. My name is Ebaide. On the 15th of October, 2021, I left my country, Nigeria, to tour 5 countries in East Africa. I planned to spend one month in each country, and decided to start in Rwanda. I flew into Nairobi Kenya on the 15th of November with an already-purchased flight ticket to Tanzania for the 13th of December. Well, guess what? I never left Kenya until the 6th of March, 2022. That day, I left Nairobi for Kampala, spent only 5 days there, and traveled back to Nigeria. I spent 2 months selling all my things and putting things in order. In May 2021, I left Nigeria with a luggage containing everything I had in the world.

Before Van Life

I became fully independent at 17. Since then, I’ve worked many jobs to see myself through school, and be the best in every endeavor I took. My every day quest was to earn more money, get the next promotion, the next bigger gig. I would say I wasn’t doing bad in Lagos. I worked hard enough to afford to buy a nice car, to shoot films for my production company, wear some designer shoes, tour some countries, and live just a tad above average in the Nigerian economy. Sometimes, I spent up to 14 hours working, other times, I spent 10. My highly competitive spirit made it easy to put in the extra hours I needed to be the best in every where I worked. I was the type who would sleep while working and wake up into it. But then, that was never enough for me. I simply wasn’t contented living my life on pre-plans, on routines. Having life pass me by while living it conventionally and safely made me miserable. I wanted more. I always had this deep-set unsettling feeling because I knew there was more out there for me. I knew I’d rather be cramped up on a tiny bed in a $7 hotel than a 5-star hotel. I wanted to get my shoes dirty while hiking or spend 20 straight hours sitting on a bus from country A to B.

They said the grass isn’t always greener on the other side. For me, I tested the waters enough to know that the grass is definitely greener for the alternative lifestyle I earnestly craved. I moved to Kenya for greener pastures, to have my basic needs sorted so I could have the time and the mental stability to pursue things that would improve my life. I moved to Nairobi, and rented a beautiful apartment on the 6th floor of a high-rise building. My happiness in that house lasted for only 3 months. I started feeling uncomfortable with having to pay my rent every 30 days. You see, we pay yearly in Nigeria, and I was used to that. Not only were Nairobi apartments thrice more expensive that Lagos, I had two part with money for rent 12 times in a year. Traveling around Kenya became hard as I had to pay for an Airbnb or hotel while travelling and still had to keep paying for my apartment in Nairobi. Traveling stopped being nearly as much fun as it was because it meant that I had to pay for two apartments at the same time. I couldn’t help feeling that my life would be much better if I spent that money making memories on the road.

Van Life Story

I stumbled upon van life about 5 years ago on YouTube. I conceived the idea of doing it, but never took it seriously because I always knew I wouldn’t be able to do it in my country, Nigeria. No way would I have felt safe enough. During the pandemic, I watched so many van life vlogs that I started living the van life dream in my dreams! I conceived the idea of buying a Kombi, converting it to suit my lifestyle and driving to beaches wearing the sexiest bikinis. Problem was, I knew it wouldn’t happen any time soon because I lived in a country that wouldn’t have been safe enough for a solo female traveler living in a van on the streets. One month after I moved to kenya, I went solo camping in a place called Paradise Lost in Kiambu road, Nairobi. I was alone in the forest over the night. The sound of the forest left me petrified all night, but the next day, I felt so safe that I didn’t want to leave. That was when I knew Van life could be something I’ll enjoy doing in Kenya. It had always been someday, I’d live an alternative lifestyle. Someday, I’ll hit the road full-time. Someday, I’ll build a home on wheels from scratch. Someday, I’ll own a home that’d take me to the wildest of places. By the end of June, I’d had so many sleepless nights that I decided MY SOMEDAY WAS GOING TO BE RIGHT NOW. It took me a while to get the right van as it was a spontaneous decision. I needed a shell van with a great engine, something I could easily tear down and build into my desired tiny home. On the 17th of September, 2022, I bought my 1987 Nissan Vannette. We bought it at Narok and drove it to Nairobi, kenya. We spent three months building. On the 4th of January, 2023, I moved into Daddy, the van with the things I hadn’t sold. On the 5th of January, 2023, we took a 7-hour trip from Nairobi to Meru where I started my van life journey.

My plans

I could write several articles detailing how excellent van life is, how this is the best decision I’ve ever made, how you should sell your things and move into a moving vehicle, how it would be the highlight of your life, but… I WON’T. Instead, I want you to live vicariously through the tribulations, excitement, pain, conquests and highlights I’d experience while living on the road. I want you to feel the joy I feel from finding the most beautiful places and feel my pain when my van breaks down at the times adventures go wrong. I want you to see what it truly feels like to live in a vehicle. I want to share the laughter and tears with you. I believe that’s the only way you can truly get a feel of what Van Life truly is. THERE’D BE ZERO Inspirational blogs Boring lists Glitz and glamour *Inspirational bullshit irks me: I’m not the next blogger to tell you how my life is perfect after I sold everything and started traveling the world with a backpack and $20 in my pocket. No, $20 covers nothing. Traveling is expensive. If you don’t have some money saved or a sustainable job on the road, stay in your house. DO NOT get on the road. It’s a rewarding but hard life. If you believe that crap about leaving the life you know behind with no plans or money, YOU WILL SUFFER. *No boring lists: I don’t do perfect traveling. I want to experience the frustrations, the highs and lows. I want to experience it ALL. I won’t be the 954th blogger to tell you the best places to visit in Kenya. I won’t spam you with blogs about places that already saturate the Internet. Most of the lists I will be making are of things a solo female loner does while living and traveling in her van. *Zero Glitz & Glamour: I don’t do makeup. I don’t make frequent salon visits. I won’t make blogs about the fanciest hotels and the most expensive tourist sites. I am a mess who sits on the floor of her van and has been carrying the same old backpack every day since 2021. I’ll take you to the roughest places, do a lot of bush crafting, meet tribes and people. Social media is full of people putting more importance to superficial things and never finding the time to actually enjoy moments. People care more about what they’re wearing and their made-up face than than truly experiencing moments that leave everlasting imprints in their lives

Ebaide Today

At the time of opening this website and writing this blog, I am in Meru, kenya. I am starting my van life journey here because it is slower than Nairobi. I thought it would be easier for me to get into this lifestyle here than in a fast place. So far, I really enjoy living in my space. Daddy, the van is MINE. It is MY thing, MY own. I have never felt freer than this. I am still at the teething stage, but I’m sure time will help blend me more into this lifestyle. My YouTube channel will always be busy with new videos. This blog will always have new stories about a Nigerian girl living in a small van away from her home country.
